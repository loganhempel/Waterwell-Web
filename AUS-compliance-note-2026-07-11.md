## 1. Who actually governs this (plain English)

Four regimes matter. None of them is "the naturopath regulator" because Australia doesn't have one.

### TGA — Therapeutic Goods Administration
Regulates advertising of **therapeutic goods** (medicines, supplements, herbal products, devices) to Australians, under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989 and the Therapeutic Goods (Advertising) Code (the 2021 instrument, still the current Code as of mid-2026). The TGA **does not regulate the promotion of health services**. But — and this is the trap — an ad for a service becomes an ad for therapeutic goods the moment it refers, directly or indirectly, to the goods used in the service. TGA's own guidance: to keep a health-service ad out of scope, "it is best not to refer to any therapeutic goods used in the delivery of the service in the advertisement." Their worked example of a SAFE ad: *"Call our clinic for a consultation to discuss treatment options for migraine"* — mentions a condition, mentions a consultation, mentions no goods.
Sources: [TGA — Advertising a health service](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/advertising-health-service) · [TGA — Advertising health services that involve therapeutic goods](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/advertising-health-services-involve-therapeutic-goods) · [TGA — Applying the Advertising Code](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/regulations-all-products/advertising/applying-advertising-code)

**Cross-border note:** the Act covers advertising of therapeutic goods "in Australia" across all media including online, and the TGA actively monitors online content and works with digital platforms on breaches. I could not find an explicit TGA statement on enforcement against a purely NZ-based advertiser with no AU presence — practical enforcement against Kohei personally may be limited, but Google will still disapprove non-compliant ads, and the safe operating assumption is that ads targeted at Australian consumers are treated as advertising in Australia. **Flagged as uncertain — behave as if it applies.**
Source: [TGA — Advertising legal framework](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/regulations-all-products/advertising/advertising-legal-framework)

### Ahpra / National Law s.133 — mostly does NOT apply
Naturopathy is **not one of the Ahpra-registered professions**. It remains unregistered as of 2026 — the profession lodged a formal submission seeking statutory registration in March 2026, but nothing has been granted. So s.133 of the National Law (which bans testimonials, misleading claims etc. for *regulated* health services) does not technically bind Kohei — **unless** he also holds an Ahpra registration in some other profession, in which case Ahpra says the advertising rules apply to ALL his advertising, including the naturopathy side. Confirm Kohei holds no AU registration.
Sources: [ANPA — Professional Regulation](https://anpa.asn.au/professional-regulation/) · [Australian Naturopathic Council — registration submission, March 2026](https://www.naturopathiccouncil.org.au/2026/03/27/formal-submission-to-government-seeking-statutory-registration-finalised-march-2026/) · [Ahpra — Advertising guidelines](https://www.ahpra.gov.au/Resources/Advertising-hub/Advertising-guidelines-and-other-guidance/Advertising-guidelines.aspx)

**Don't celebrate.** Even though s.133 doesn't bind him, Ahpra's rules are the de facto standard regulators and platforms benchmark against, and the two regimes below fill the gap.

### National Code of Conduct for Health Care Workers (the unregistered-practitioner code)
This is the regime that actually targets naturopaths. Agreed by COAG in 2015, administered state-by-state (NSW Health Care Complaints Commission, Qld Office of the Health Ombudsman, SA, Vic, etc.). Key advertising clause: a health care worker **must not make claims, in advertising or promotional material, about the efficacy of treatment or services if those claims cannot be substantiated**. Breach can lead to prohibition orders that ban practising or advertising to residents of that state.
Sources: [Qld OHO — National Code of Conduct](https://www.oho.qld.gov.au/for-providers/national-code-of-conduct-for-health-care-workers) · [NHAA — Guide to the Code of Conduct for Unregistered Healthcare Practitioners](https://nhaa.org.au/wp-content/uploads/NHAA-2021-Guide-to-the-Code-of-Conduct-for-Unregistered-Healthcare-Practitioenrs_Final_.pdf) · [SA Health — Unregistered health practitioners](https://www.sahealth.sa.gov.au/wps/wcm/connect/public+content/sa+health+internet/clinical+resources/education+and+training/system+improvement/unregistered+health+practitioners)

**Cross-border note:** whether a state code binds a NZ practitioner delivering telehealth into that state is genuinely unsettled — the codes are drafted around services "provided in" the state, and telehealth to a NSW resident arguably counts. **Unverified. Treat the substantiation rule as binding anyway** — it costs nothing to comply and the ACL (below) definitely applies.

### Australian Consumer Law (ACL) — definitely applies
ACL s.18 bans misleading or deceptive conduct in trade or commerce; ss.29/33 ban false representations about services. The ACL reaches **overseas businesses carrying on business with Australian consumers** — settled in *ACCC v Valve Corp* (Full Federal Court 2017, US company, AU consumers, $3M penalty). A NZ clinic selling consultations to Australians is squarely in scope. The ACCC has run health-claims enforcement jointly flagged with TGA and Ahpra.
Sources: [ACCC — False or misleading claims](https://www.accc.gov.au/business/advertising-and-promotions/false-or-misleading-claims) · [MinterEllison — TGA/ACCC/Ahpra enforcement roundup](https://www.minterellison.com/articles/unlawful-pharma-and-health-service-advertising-in-regulators-sights)

**Bottom line:** for Waterwell the operative rules are **TGA Code (only if ads/landing pages mention goods) + National Code substantiation rule + ACL + Google's own policies**. Ahpra is out of frame unless Kohei registers in an Ahpra profession.

## 2. Google Ads healthcare policy (the gate that actually blocks unpause)

- **Healthcare is a sensitive category.** Consultation-service ads generally don't need certification, but content restrictions apply regardless. Source: [Google — Healthcare and medicines policy](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/176031?hl=en-AU)
- **Telemedicine certification risk (the big one):** Google requires telemedicine providers advertising to Australia to hold LegitScript Healthcare Merchant Certification (plus Google certification). Whether a naturopath doing online consults counts as "telemedicine" under Google's definition is not spelled out — the policy is aimed mostly at prescribing services. **Unverified either way. This must be resolved before unpause** — either Google confirms out-of-scope, or the ads pass review cleanly without a certification disapproval, or Kohei gets LegitScript-certified. Sources: [Google policy](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/176031?hl=en-AU) · [LegitScript telemedicine certification](https://www.legitscript.com/certification/telemedicine/) · [ppc.land — Google AU healthcare policy update](https://ppc.land/google-updates-healthcare-and-medicines-policy-for-australian-advertisers/)
- **No prescription drug terms** in ad copy or landing pages for AU-targeted campaigns (unless certified). Irrelevant to naturopathy content, but a stray "instead of antidepressants" style line would trip it.
- **Speculative/experimental medical treatments are prohibited** outright. Naturopathy consults as such are not banned, but any specific therapy pitched with unproven treatment claims (e.g. "hair mineral analysis diagnoses X", "homeopathic treatment for Y") risks disapproval under this or the Misrepresentation policy. Source: [Google policy](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/176031?hl=en-AU)
- **Personalised advertising restrictions:** health is a sensitive category — **no remarketing/retargeting and no Customer Match** for ads about treatment of medical conditions. Site visitors cannot be retargeted with condition-related ads. Build AU campaigns as search-intent only. Source: [Google — Health in personalized advertising](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/16701855?hl=en)

## 3. ALLOWED in ad copy and landing pages

Keep every ad a **service ad, not a goods ad**, with substantiable, non-serious claims.

- Naming the service and modality: "Naturopathic consultations, online across Australia."
- Practitioner credentials, honestly stated: "NZ-registered naturopath" (say NZ — implying AU registration that doesn't exist breaches the National Code and ACL).
- Conditions addressed, in non-serious form, with soft verbs: "Support for digestion, energy and sleep" / "Book a consultation to discuss your fatigue" — mirrors the TGA's own safe example ("Call our clinic for a consultation to discuss treatment options for migraine").
- Process/logistics: consult length, telehealth format, pricing, booking link, "initial consult $X".
- General wellbeing language: "personalised health plans", "holistic assessment", "work one-on-one with a naturopath".
- Factual, substantiable statements: years in practice, qualifications held, association memberships (e.g. NZ professional body — named accurately).
- Testimonials about **service experience only** (friendliness, clarity, felt listened to) with no clinical/outcome content and no product mentions — legal for an unregistered practitioner, but see the risk note in section 4; safest is none at all in AU-targeted assets.

## 4. BANNED / HIGH-RISK in ad copy and landing pages

- **Naming or hinting at therapeutic goods** in ads or AU landing pages: specific supplements, herbal medicines ("we prescribe iron + B12", "herbal tonics for stress"). Even "practitioner-only supplements included" drags the ad into the TGA Code — full compliance burden, permitted-indications limits, testimonial ban. [TGA guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/advertising-health-service)
- **Testimonials about therapeutic goods** — banned outright under Code s.24 where the ad is in TGA scope; endorsements by health practitioners (including Kohei himself endorsing products) are also prohibited (s.24(6)). [TGA — Testimonials and endorsements](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/regulations-all-products/advertising/applying-advertising-code/testimonials-and-endorsements-advertising)
- **Outcome testimonials generally** ("cured my IBS after 3 sessions") — even if s.133 doesn't bind, these are unsubstantiable efficacy claims under the National Code and misleading-conduct bait under ACL s.18. Google also dislikes them. Treat as banned.
- **Cure/treat/prevent claims for serious conditions** (restricted representations): diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, depression, heart disease, etc. require prior TGA approval when in TGA scope, and are unsubstantiable claims regardless. "Treat your depression naturally" is a compliance grenade. [TGA — Restricted and prohibited representations](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/regulations-all-products/advertising/applying-advertising-code/restricted-and-prohibited-representations-advertising)
- **Prohibited representations — never, no approval available in practice:** cancer, STIs, HIV/AIDS, hepatitis C, mental illness. Zero references in any AU-facing asset. Same source.
- **Before/after implications** — "from chronic fatigue to full energy in 8 weeks", transformation imagery, case-study arcs. Functions as an outcome claim + testimonial; unsubstantiable.
- **"Detox" claims** — "detox programs", "cleanse your liver", "flush toxins". High-risk: implies physiological effect with no accepted evidence base (ACL/National Code), usually implies goods (teas, powders → TGA scope), and pattern-matches Google's unproven-claims filters.
- **Anti-medicine positioning** — "natural alternative to medication", drug names, "get off your meds". ACL risk, prescription-term risk with Google AU, and public-health red flag.
- **Guarantees and speed claims** — "results guaranteed", "feel better in 2 weeks", "works where doctors failed".
- **"Scientifically proven" without holding the actual evidence file** — substantiation must exist before the claim runs, not after a complaint.
- **Implying AU registration or Ahpra status** — Kohei has none; National Code and ACL both prohibit claiming registration you don't hold.

## 5. AU landing-page checklist

The ad's landing page is legally part of the ad (TGA and Google both treat it that way). Before unpause, the AU page (`au.html` or successor) must have:

- [ ] Zero product/supplement/herb/brand names anywhere on the page (audit body copy, alt text, meta description, FAQ, footer)
- [ ] Zero testimonials with clinical outcomes or product mentions; ideally zero testimonials
- [ ] No serious-condition claims (no cancer/mental illness/STI/hep C references at all; no diabetes/depression/arthritis "treatment" language)
- [ ] No "detox/cleanse/toxins" language
- [ ] No before/after stories, imagery, or "results in X weeks" framing
- [ ] Credentials block states NZ registration explicitly ("NZ-registered naturopath") and does not imply Australian registration
- [ ] Clear disclosure that consultations are delivered online from New Zealand
- [ ] A "naturopathy complements, does not replace, medical care — see your GP for diagnosis" line
- [ ] Pricing in AUD or clearly flagged as NZD
- [ ] Every efficacy-ish claim on the page has a substantiation file behind it (source saved, dated) — or the claim is cut
- [ ] Privacy policy covers AU visitors (Privacy Act 1988 APPs apply to businesses collecting Australians' data — flag for later, low urgency at consult-booking scale)
- [ ] No prescription drug names anywhere on the page
- [ ] Conversion tracking present and firing (standard Emporom gate — currently unbuilt on Waterwell AU per July status)

## 7. Open uncertainties (honest list)

1. **TGA reach over a NZ-only advertiser** — not explicitly confirmed; assumed to apply to AU-targeted ads. Practical risk is mostly Google disapproval + ACCC, not TGA prosecution.
2. **State National Code applicability to inbound telehealth from NZ** — unsettled; complying costs nothing so we comply.
3. **Google's "telemedicine" definition** vs a naturopath video consult — unresolved; explicitly gated above.
4. **Naturopath registration in AU** — still unregistered as of July 2026, but a formal registration submission went to governments in March 2026. If registration lands, Ahpra s.133 (testimonial ban etc.) starts binding. Re-check this note every ~6 months.
5. **The Code itself** — the Therapeutic Goods (Advertising) Code 2021 instrument is current as of this research; the "new code from 1 April 2026" that appears in searches is **New Zealand's ASA code**, not Australia's. Don't confuse them.
